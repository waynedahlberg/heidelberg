  It is not down in any map; true places never are.
</Epigraph>

<p className="flush drop-cap">What the white whale was to Ahab, has been hinted; what, at times, he was to me, as yet remains unsaid. Aside from those more obvious considerations touching Moby Dick, which could not but occasionally awaken in any man’s soul some alarm, there was another thought, or rather vague, nameless horror concerning him, which at times by its intensity completely overpowered all the rest; and yet so mystical and well nigh ineffable was it, that I almost despair of putting it in a comprehensible form.<Sidenote>Chapter 42 was singled out by D.&#8202;H. Lawrence as the imaginative center of the book: “a wonderful chapter... the most curious chapter in a curious book.”</Sidenote> It was the whiteness of the whale that above all things appalled me. But how can I hope to explain myself here; and yet, in some dim, random way, explain myself I must, else all these chapters might be naught.</p>

Though in many natural objects, whiteness refiningly enhances beauty, as if imparting some special virtue of its own, as in marbles, japonicas, and pearls;<Sidenote>Melville’s catalogues — marbles, japonicas, pearls — are the rhetorical engine of the chapter: accumulation in place of argument.</Sidenote> and though various nations have in some way recognised a certain royal preeminence in this hue; and though, besides all this, whiteness has been even made significant of gladness, for among the Romans a white stone marked a joyful day; yet for all these accumulated associations, with whatever is sweet, and honorable, and sublime, there yet lurks an elusive something in the innermost idea of this hue, which strikes more of panic to the soul than that redness which affrights in blood.

<PullQuote>
  <p>It was the whiteness of the whale that above all things appalled me.</p>
</PullQuote>

<p className="flush"><Newthought>This elusive quality it is,</Newthought> which causes the thought of whiteness, when divorced from more kindly associations, and coupled with any object terrible in itself, to heighten that terror to the furthest bounds. Witness the white bear of the poles, and the white shark of the tropics; what but their smooth, flaky whiteness makes them the transcendent horrors they are? That ghastly whiteness it is which imparts such an abhorrent mildness, even more loathsome than terrific, to the dumb gloating of their aspect.<MarginNote>The polar bear and the white shark share a page here with the albatross, the White Steed of the prairies, and the Albino man — a bestiary of pallor.</MarginNote> So that not the fierce-fanged tiger in his heraldic coat can so stagger courage as the white-shrouded bear or shark.</p>

Bethink thee of the albatross, whence come those clouds of spiritual wonderment and pale dread, in which that white phantom sails in all imaginations? Not Coleridge first threw that spell; but God’s great, unflattering laureate, Nature.

<SectionBreak />

<p className="flush"><Newthought>But not yet have we solved</Newthought> the incantation of this whiteness, and learned why it appeals with such power to the soul; and more strange and far more portentous — why, as we have seen, it is at once the most meaning symbol of spiritual things, nay, the very veil of the Christian’s Deity; and yet should be as it is, the intensifying agent in things the most appalling to mankind.</p>

Is it that by its indefiniteness it shadows forth the heartless voids and immensities of the universe, and thus stabs us from behind with the thought of annihilation, when beholding the white depths of the milky way? Or is it, that as in essence whiteness is not so much a color as the visible absence of color, and at the same time the concrete of all colors; is it for these reasons that there is such a dumb blankness, full of meaning, in a wide landscape of snows — a colorless, all-color of atheism from which we shrink?

<Blockquote author="Herman Melville" work="Moby-Dick, ch. 42" year="1851">
  And of all these things the Albino whale was the symbol. Wonder ye then at the fiery hunt?
</Blockquote>

<p className="flush">A note for the typographically-minded reader: it is no accident that this chapter suits a specimen page. An essay on whiteness is an essay on the ground against which every figure is seen — what printers call the color of the page. Bringhurst’s first duty of the typographer is to preserve that even gray; Melville’s chapter is a meditation on what happens when the ground itself becomes the terror.</p>
