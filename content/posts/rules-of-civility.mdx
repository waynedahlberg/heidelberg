<p className="flush drop-cap">Before he was a surveyor, a general, or a president, George Washington was a schoolboy with a copybook. Sometime around 1744 — he was about fifteen — he filled the last ten pages of a slim notebook with one hundred and ten precepts under the title <em>Rules of Civility &amp; Decent Behaviour in Company and Conversation</em>. The manuscript survives in the Library of Congress: stained, rodent-nibbled at the edges, and still one of the most revealing documents of his youth.<Sidenote>The notebook also holds sample deeds, leases, and a tip for keeping ink from freezing in winter — “add a few drops of brandy.” The Rules sat among tools of practical advancement, not among idle exercises.</Sidenote></p>

## Where the Rules came from

<p className="flush"><Newthought>Washington did not invent them.</Newthought> Nearly every precept descends from a French Jesuit courtesy book of 1595, <em>Bienséance de la conversation entre les hommes</em>. An English version, translated by the child prodigy Francis Hawkins and published about 1640 as <em>Youths Behaviour</em>, circulated for a century in Britain and the colonies. What the young Virginian wrote out was a penmanship exercise — and a curriculum. In a colony perched between drawing-room gentility and frontier roughness, manners were a technology of self-making.</p>

Some historians caution against reading too much into a schoolboy’s copy. Yet the earnestness of the hand is hard to miss. Rules about spitting, nail-chewing, and greasy knives are the sort of reminders a tall, ambitious youth might think he needed. Decades later, biographers would hear in these pages the grain of the public Washington: punctilious, reserved, exacting of his officers, courteous down the ranks.

## What the one hundred and ten contain

The Rules are not a random heap. Read in order, they move outward from the body to the company, then inward again to character and conscience:

1. **Presence of the body** — how one sits, stands, coughs, and occupies space.
2. **Respect, rank, and ceremony** — doors, walking order, hats, and deference.
3. **Character in company** — flattery, mockery, promises, and companions.
4. **Speech and discourse** — listening, brevity, and speech about the absent.
5. **At table** — the small theatre of knife, salt, napkin, and temper.
6. **Conscience** — reverence, recreation, and the spark that ends the list.

What follows is the complete text, lightly regularized in spelling where the manuscript or early print is merely eccentric, arranged under those heads. The numbering is Washington’s.

<PullQuote>
  <p>Associate yourself with Men of good Quality if you Esteem your own Reputation; for ’tis better to be alone than in bad Company.</p>
</PullQuote>


## Presence of the body

The first rules concern the body in company: how one sits, stands, coughs, fidgets, and occupies a room without becoming a spectacle.

<ol start={1}>
  <li>Every Action done in Company, ought to be with Some Sign of Respect, to those that are Present.</li>
  <li>When in Company, put not your Hands to any Part of the Body, not usually Discovered.</li>
  <li>Show Nothing to your Friend that may affright him.</li>
  <li>In the Presence of Others Sing not to yourself with a humming Noise, nor Drum with your Fingers or Feet.</li>
  <li>If You Cough, Sneeze, Sigh, or Yawn, do it not Loud but Privately; and Speak not in your Yawning, but put Your handkerchief or Hand before your face and turn aside.</li>
  <li>Sleep not when others Speak, Sit not when others stand, Speak not when you Should hold your Peace, walk not on when others Stop.</li>
  <li>Put not off your Clothes in the presence of Others, nor go out of your Chamber half Dressed.</li>
  <li>At Play and at Fire it's Good manners to Give Place to the last Comer, and affect not to Speak Louder than Ordinary.</li>
  <li>Spit not in the Fire, nor Stoop low before it neither Put your Hands into the Flames to warm them, nor Set your Feet upon the Fire especially if there be meat before it.</li>
  <li>When you Sit down, Keep your Feet firm and Even, without putting one on the other or Crossing them.</li>
  <li>Shift not yourself in the Sight of others nor Gnaw your nails.</li>
  <li>Shake not the head, Feet, or Legs roll not the Eyes lift not one eyebrow higher than the other wry not the mouth, and bedew no man's face with your Spittle, by approaching too near him when you Speak.</li>
  <li>Kill no Vermin as Fleas, lice ticks etc. in the Sight of Others, if you See any filth or thick Spittle put your foot Dexterously upon it if it be upon the Clothes of your Companions, Put it off privately, and if it be upon your own Clothes return Thanks to him who puts it off.</li>
  <li>Turn not your Back to others especially in Speaking, Jog not the Table or Desk on which Another reads or writes, lean not upon any one.</li>
  <li>Keep your Nails clean and Short, also your Hands and Teeth Clean yet without Showing any great Concern for them.</li>
  <li>Do not Puff up the Cheeks, Loll not out the tongue rub the Hands, or beard, thrust out the lips, or bite them or keep the Lips too open or too Closed.</li>
</ol>

## Respect, rank, and ceremony

Colonial Virginia was a world of degrees. Washington’s copybook teaches when to rise, whom to yield to at a door, and how to place oneself when walking with superiors.

<ol start={17}>
  <li>Be no Flatterer, neither Play with any that delights not to be Played Withal.</li>
  <li>Read no Letters, Books, or Papers in Company but when there is a Necessity for the doing of it you must ask leave: come not near the Books or Writings of Another so as to read them unless desired or give your opinion of them unasked also look not nigh when another is writing a Letter.</li>
  <li>Let your Countenance be pleasant but in Serious Matters Somewhat grave.</li>
  <li>The Gestures of the Body must be Suited to the discourse you are upon.</li>
  <li>Reproach none for the Infirmities of Nature, nor Delight to Put them that have in mind thereof.</li>
  <li>Show not yourself glad at the Misfortune of another though he were your enemy.</li>
  <li>When you see a Crime punished, you may be inwardly Pleased; but always show Pity to the Suffering Offender.</li>
  <li>Do not laugh too loud or too much at any Public Spectacle.</li>
  <li>Superfluous Compliments and all Affectation of Ceremony are to be avoided, yet where due they are not to be Neglected.</li>
  <li>In Pulling off your Hat to Persons of Distinction, as Noblemen, Justices, Churchmen etc. make a Reverence, bowing more or less according to the Custom of the Better Bred, and Quality of the Person. Amongst your equals expect not always that they Should begin with you first, but to Pull off the Hat when there is no need is Affectation, in the Manner of Saluting and resaluting in words keep to the most usual Custom.</li>
  <li>Tis ill manners to bid one more eminent than yourself be covered as well as not to do it to whom it's due Likewise he that makes too much haste to Put on his hat does not well, yet he ought to Put it on at the first, or at most the Second time of being asked; now what is herein Spoken, of Qualification in behavior in Saluting, ought also to be observed in taking of Place, and Sitting down for ceremonies without Bounds is troublesome.</li>
  <li>If any one come to Speak to you while you are Sitting Stand up though he be your Inferior, and when you Present Seats let it be to every one according to his Degree.</li>
  <li>When you meet with one of Greater Quality than yourself, Stop, and retire especially if it be at a Door or any Straight place to give way for him to Pass.</li>
  <li>In walking the highest Place in most Countries Seems to be on the right hand therefore Place yourself on the left of him whom you desire to Honour: but if three walk together the midst Place is the most Honourable the wall is usually given to the most worthy if two walk together.</li>
  <li>If any one far Surpasses others, either in age, Estate, or Merit yet would give Place to a meaner than himself in his own lodging or elsewhere the one ought not to accept it, So he on the other part should not use much earnestness nor offer it above once or twice.</li>
  <li>To one that is your equal, or not much inferior you are to give the chief Place in your Lodging and he to who 'tis offered ought at the first to refuse it but at the Second to accept though not without acknowledging his own unworthiness.</li>
  <li>They that are in Dignity or in office have in all places Precedency but whilst they are Young they ought to respect those that are their equals in Birth or other Qualities, though they have no Public charge.</li>
  <li>It is good Manners to prefer them to whom we Speak before ourselves especially if they be above us with whom in no Sort we ought to begin.</li>
  <li>Let your Discourse with Men of Business be Short and Comprehensive.</li>
  <li>Artificers & Persons of low Degree ought not to use many ceremonies to Lords, or Others of high Degree but Respect and highly Honour them, and those of high Degree ought to treat them with affability & Courtesy, without Arrogance.</li>
  <li>In Speaking to men of Quality do not lean nor Look them full in the Face, nor approach too near them at least Keep a full Pace from them.</li>
  <li>In visiting the Sick, do not Presently play the Physician if you be not Knowing therein.</li>
  <li>In writing or Speaking, give to every Person his due Title According to his Degree & the Custom of the Place.</li>
  <li>Strive not with your Superiors in argument, but always Submit your Judgment to others with Modesty.</li>
  <li>Undertake not to Teach your equal in the art himself Professes; it Savours of arrogance.</li>
  <li>Let thy ceremonies in Courtesy be proper to the Dignity of his place with whom thou converses for it is absurd to act the same with a Clown and a Prince.</li>
  <li>Do not express Joy before one sick or in pain for that contrary Passion will aggravate his Misery.</li>
  <li>When a man does all he can though it Succeeds not well blame not him that did it.</li>
  <li>Being to advise or reprehend any one, consider whether it ought to be in public or in Private; presently, or at Some other time in what terms to do it & in reproving Show no Sign of Choler but do it with all Sweetness and Mildness.</li>
</ol>

## Character in company

Beyond gesture lies character: flattery, mockery, curiosity, promise-keeping, and the company one keeps.

<ol start={46}>
  <li>Take all Admonitions thankfully in what Time or Place Soever given but afterwards not being culpable take a Time & Place convenient to let him know it that gave them.</li>
  <li>Mock not nor Jest at any thing of Importance break no Jests that are Sharp Biting and if you Deliver any thing witty and Pleasant abstain from Laughing thereat yourself.</li>
  <li>Wherein you reprove Another be unblameable yourself; for example is more prevalent than Precepts.</li>
  <li>Use no Reproachful Language against any one neither Curse nor Revile.</li>
  <li>Be not hasty to believe flying Reports to the Disparagement of any.</li>
  <li>Wear not your Clothes, foul, ripped or Dusty but See they be Brushed once every day at least and take heed that you approach not to any Uncleanness.</li>
  <li>In your Apparel be Modest and endeavour to accommodate Nature, rather than to procure Admiration keep to the Fashion of your equals Such as are Civil and orderly with respect to Times and Places.</li>
  <li>Run not in the Streets, neither go too slowly nor with Mouth open go not Shaking your Arms kick not the earth with your feet, go not upon the Toes, nor in a Dancing fashion.</li>
  <li>Play not the Peacock, looking everywhere about you, to See if you be well Decked, if your Shoes fit well if your Stockings sit neatly, and Clothes handsomely.</li>
  <li>Eat not in the Streets, nor in the House, out of Season.</li>
  <li>Associate yourself with Men of good Quality if you Esteem your own Reputation; for 'tis better to be alone than in bad Company.</li>
  <li>In walking up and Down in a House, only with One in Company if he be Greater than yourself, at the first give him the Right hand and Stop not till he does and be not the first that turns, and when you do turn let it be with your face towards him, if he be a Man of Great Quality, walk not with him Cheek by Jowl but Somewhat behind him; but yet in Such a Manner that he may easily Speak to you.</li>
  <li>Let your Conversation be without Malice or Envy, for 'tis a Sign of a Tractable and Commendable Nature: And in all Causes of Passion admit Reason to Govern.</li>
  <li>Never express anything unbecoming, nor Act against the Rules Moral before your inferiors.</li>
  <li>Be not immodest in urging your Friends to Discover a Secret.</li>
  <li>Utter not base and frivolous things amongst grave and Learned Men nor very Difficult Questions or Subjects, among the Ignorant or things hard to be believed, Stuff not your Discourse with Sentences amongst your Betters nor Equals.</li>
  <li>Speak not of doleful Things in a Time of Mirth or at the Table; Speak not of Melancholy Things as Death and Wounds, and if others Mention them Change if you can the Discourse tell not your Dreams, but to your intimate Friend.</li>
  <li>A Man ought not to value himself of his Achievements, or rare Qualities of wit; much less of his riches Virtue or Kindred.</li>
  <li>Break not a Jest where none take pleasure in mirth Laugh not aloud, nor at all without Occasion, deride no man's Misfortune, tho' there Seem to be Some cause.</li>
  <li>Speak not injurious Words neither in Jest nor Earnest Scoff at none although they give Occasion.</li>
  <li>Be not forward but friendly and Courteous; the first to Salute hear and answer & be not Pensive when it's a time to Converse.</li>
  <li>Detract not from others neither be excessive in Commanding.</li>
  <li>Go not thither, where you know not, whether you Shall be Welcome or not. Give not Advice without being Asked & when desired do it briefly.</li>
  <li>If two contend together take not the part of either unconstrained; and be not obstinate in your own Opinion, in Things indifferent be of the Major Side.</li>
  <li>Reprehend not the imperfections of others for that belongs to Parents Masters and Superiors.</li>
  <li>Gaze not on the marks or blemishes of Others and ask not how they came. What you may Speak in Secret to your Friend deliver not before others.</li>
</ol>

## Speech and discourse

A long middle stretch of the Rules is a manual of conversation — when to speak, how to listen, what never to say of the absent.

<ol start={72}>
  <li>Speak not in an unknown Tongue in Company but in your own Language and that as those of Quality do and not as the Vulgar; Sublime matters treat Seriously.</li>
  <li>Think before you Speak pronounce not imperfectly nor bring out your Words too hastily but orderly & distinctly.</li>
  <li>When Another Speaks be attentive your Self and disturb not the Audience if any hesitate in his Words help him not nor Prompt him without desired, Interrupt him not, nor Answer him till his Speech be ended.</li>
  <li>In the midst of Discourse ask not of what one treateth but if you Perceive any Stop because of your coming you may well entreat him gently to Proceed: If a Person of Quality comes in while you are Conversing it's handsome to Repeat what was said before.</li>
  <li>While you are talking, Point not with your Finger at him of Whom you Discourse nor Approach too near him to whom you talk especially to his face.</li>
  <li>Treat with men at fit Times about Business & Whisper not in the Company of Others.</li>
  <li>Make no Comparisons and if any of the Company be Commended for any brave act of Virtue, commend not another for the Same.</li>
  <li>Be not apt to relate News if you know not the truth thereof. In Discoursing of things you Have heard Name not your Author always A Secret Discover not.</li>
  <li>Be not Tedious in Discourse or in reading unless you find the Company pleased therewith.</li>
  <li>Be not Curious to Know the Affairs of Others neither approach those that Speak in Private.</li>
  <li>Undertake not what you cannot perform but be careful to keep your promise.</li>
  <li>When you deliver a matter do it without passion & with discretion, however mean the person be you do it to.</li>
  <li>When your Superiors talk to any Body hearken not neither Speak nor Laugh.</li>
  <li>In Company of those of Higher Quality than yourself Speak not until you are asked a Question then Stand upright put off your Hat & Answer in few words.</li>
  <li>In Disputes, be not So Desirous to Overcome as not to give Liberty to each one to deliver his Opinion and Submit to the Judgment of the Major Part especially if they are Judges of the Dispute.</li>
  <li>Let thy carriage be such as becomes a Man Grave Settled and attentive to that which is spoken. Contradict not at every turn what others Say.</li>
  <li>Be not tedious in Discourse, make not many Digressions, nor repeat often the Same manner of Discourse.</li>
  <li>Speak not Evil of the absent for it is unjust.</li>
</ol>

## At table

The table is its own theatre. Knife, salt, napkin, and temper each receive a precept.

<ol start={90}>
  <li>Being Set at meat Scratch not neither Spit Cough or blow your Nose except there's a Necessity for it.</li>
  <li>Make no Show of taking great Delight in your Victuals, Feed not with Greediness; cut your Bread with a Knife, lean not on the Table neither find fault with what you Eat.</li>
  <li>Take no Salt or cut Bread with your Knife Greasy.</li>
  <li>Entertaining any one at table it is decent to present him with meat, Undertake not to help others undesired by the Master.</li>
  <li>If you Soak bread in the Sauce let it be no more than what you put in your Mouth at a time and blow not your broth at Table but Stay till it Cools of it Self.</li>
  <li>Put not your meat to your Mouth with your Knife in your hand neither Spit forth the Stones of any fruit Pie upon a Dish nor Cast anything under the table.</li>
  <li>It's unbecoming to Stoop much to one's Meat Keep your Fingers clean & when foul wipe them on a Corner of your Table Napkin.</li>
  <li>Put not another bit into your Mouth until the former be Swallowed let not your Morsels be too big for the Jowls.</li>
  <li>Drink not nor talk with your mouth full neither Gaze about you while you are a Drinking.</li>
  <li>Drink not too leisurely nor yet too hastily. Before and after Drinking wipe your Lips breathe not then or Ever with too Great a Noise, for it's uncivil.</li>
  <li>Cleanse not your teeth with the Table Cloth Napkin Fork or Knife but if Others do it let it be done with a Pick Tooth.</li>
  <li>Rinse not your Mouth in the Presence of Others.</li>
  <li>It is out of use to call upon the Company often to Eat nor need you Drink to others every Time you Drink.</li>
  <li>In Company of your Betters be not longer in eating than they are lay not your Arm but only your hand upon the table.</li>
  <li>It belongs to the Chiefest in Company to unfold his Napkin and fall to Meat first, But he ought then to Begin in time & to Dispatch with Dexterity that the Slowest may have time allowed him.</li>
  <li>Be not Angry at Table whatever happens & if you have reason to be so, Show it not but on a Cheerful Countenance especially if there be Strangers for Good Humour makes one Dish of Meat a Feast.</li>
  <li>Set not yourself at the upper of the Table but if it Be your Due or that the Master of the house will have it So, Contend not, least you Should Trouble the Company.</li>
  <li>If others talk at Table be attentive but talk not with Meat in your Mouth.</li>
</ol>

## Conscience

The sequence closes not with etiquette but with the inner life — recreation, reverence, and the famous spark.

<ol start={108}>
  <li>When you Speak of God or his Attributes, let it be Seriously & with Reverence. Honour & Obey your Natural Parents although they be Poor.</li>
  <li>Let your Recreations be Manful not Sinful.</li>
  <li>Labour to keep alive in your Breast that Little Spark of Celestial fire Called Conscience.</li>
</ol>

<p className="flush"><Newthought>Read as etiquette alone,</Newthought> the Rules can seem quaint — a museum of greasy knives and humming noises. Read as a portrait of formation, they are sharper. They teach that civility is not decoration but attention: to the body one carries into a room, to the rank one neither flatters nor snubs, to the words one refuses to say of the absent, and at last to the little spark one is told to keep alive. Washington copied that last rule in a schoolboy’s hand. He spent a life trying, imperfectly, to live as if it were true.</p>
